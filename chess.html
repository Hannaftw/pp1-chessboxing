A breif history of Chess

The game of chess, a chessboxing cornerstone, has a rich history that spans over 1500 years. Originating in India during the 6th century, chess began as 'Chaturanga', a game representing the four divisions of the Indian army: infantry, cavalry, elephantry, and chariotry. This early form of chess was a strategic, two-player war game reflecting the complexities of battle.

It was in Europe during the Middle Ages that chess underwent significant transformations, with modifications to rules and design that led to the version of chess we are familiar with. Chess has historically permeated all levels of society, symbolising strategic thinking and intellectual prowess. The appeal of chess lies in its blend of simple rules and complex strategies, making it a fitting intellectual counterpart to the physical demands of boxing, truly testing both mind and body.

Algorithms can analyse games, identify weaknesses, and suggest tailored methods for improvement. The objective of Chess

Chess is played on a square board in an eight-by-eight grid. One player plays with black pieces and the other with white pieces, the player that plays white always starts the game. Players begins with 16 pieces: one king, one queen, two rooks, two knights, two bishops, and eight pawns. Each of the six piece types moves differently, with the most powerful being the queen and the least powerful the pawn. To learn about the different pieces and how they move as well as a more in depth explanation of the rules, you can visit sites such as chess.com or chesscademy.com .

The objective of the game, how you win, is by checkmate, meaning the opponent's king is placed under an inescapable threat of capture. To achieve this, a player's pieces are used to attack and capture the opponent's pieces. If there is not a checkmate, a player wins the game if the opponent resigns, or runs out of time (in timed games). A game can also end in a draw in several ways, some of which are mentioned below.

Some chess terms explained

  • FIDE - The World Chess Federation, (the acronym comes from the French "Fédération Internationale des Échecs"), is an international organization that connects the various national chess federations around the world and acts as the governing body of international chess competition. It is responsible for the rules of chess, the rating system and giving players titles depending on their official FIDE rating.
  • Check - When a player's king is under attack by at least one of the opponent's pieces. The player must get out of check on the next move, otherwise the game is lost.
  • Checkmate - The term "checkmate" in chess comes from the Persian phrase "Shah Mat," which means "the King is helpless" or "the King is ambushed". The medieval Latin version of this phrase was "scaccus mat," which directly led to the English "checkmate". It signifies a situation where the king is in a position to be captured (in check) and cannot escape to any other square, leading to the end of the game.
  • Chess ratings - there is a differentiation between online and over-the-board ratings. FIDE rating is calculated based on over-the-board performances in rated tournaments. Ratings start from 1000 and can go above 2800 for elite players. Online ratings are completely separate from the FIDE ratings and they do not affect each other. Chess websites have their own rating systems for online play, these are usually specific to platforms and can vary significantly. They may include different categories like blitz, rapid, bullet, and daily/classical games.
  • Grandmaster - The highest title a chess player can attain awarded by the world chess federation FIDE. In order to be given this title a player must reach a rating of at least 2500 and achieve three grandmaster norms. The requirements for a GM norm include: a rating of at least 2600 in the tournament, playing against a certain number of other titled players, including a minimum number of existing Grandmasters, competing in a tournament that meets specific criteria for international diversity, such as having players from multiple chess federations and achieving this level of performance in a minimum number of games, typically 9. When these requirements are met, the player is said to have "made a norm", and the tournament is called a "norm tournament". After achieving the required norms and rating, the player's application for the Grandmaster title is submitted to FIDE for official approval.
  • Stalemate - When the player whose turn it is to move has no legal move and is not in check. The game ends in a draw.
  • Fifty-move rule - If in the previous 50 moves by each player, no pawn has been moved and no capture has been made, the game ends in a draw.
  • Threefold repetition - If the same position occurs three times with the same player to move, the game ends in a draw.
  • Chess clock - A clock that is used to keep track of the time used by the players. In chessboxing the clock is one that is adapted for speed chess and is set to 3 minutes for each player.

Bobby Fisher - some "interesting" facts

Bobby Fisher is known for his outstanding chess skills but there are several things that people are often not aware of. Some interesting facts: he is the youngest player ever to earn the title "grandmaster" at the age of 15, he developed a variant of chess known as "Fischer Random Chess" or "Chess960" to emphasize skill over memorized opening sequences, he was anoutspoken anti-semite and wore anti-semitic symbols, such as a pin on his lapel (even during chess games), he was notoriously difficult to deal with with many "quirks", he often stated that he hated america and refused to play matches even after the president himself would plead with him. He was quite the character with strong opinions and no problem expressing them. Here are some of the quotes uttered by the chess legend, and yes he actually did say these things...

Chess is war over the board. The object is to crush the opponent's mind. I like the moment when I break a man's ego. I don't believe in psychology. I believe in good moves. I object to being called a chess genius because I consider myself to be and all around genius who just happens to play chess, which is rather different. I'm the most famous, the most studied, the most uncompromising, the most difficult, the most brilliant, and the most important chess player in the history of the game. A piece of garbage like Kasparov might be called a chess genius, but he's like an idiot savant. Outside of chess he knows nothing. People have been playing against me below their strength for fifteen years. My opponents make good moves too. Sometimes I don't take these things into consideration. I add status to any tournament I attend. Is it against the law to kill a reporter?

Something tells me that this man would not do very well in a game of chessboxing, but surely many people would want to meet up with him in the ring. Maybe a genious like him wouldn't stoop so low, seeing as he believed "blitz chess kills your ideas".

Learn more about chess

If you want to learn more about chess, there are countless resources available. In many places there are local chess clubs providing lessons and the chance to practice "over-the-board" chess, there are also many books on the subject, many of them are written a while back but as relevant still... but I am guessing that you will mostly be interested in the resources found online. Here are some of the best websites for learning about and refining your skills in chess:

Chess.com: a site regarded as one of the best places to learn chess, it offers a free portal where you can play chess and access a complete learning section. This includes lessons on how the game works, how the different pieces move, and insights into the game's unique mechanisms. A premium membership is avaliable that provides access to more advanced lessons and features, such as video tutorials and a chess mentorship program.
Lichess: the site is known for being a free and open-source platform. It focuses on providing a seamless user experience with interactive lessons, puzzles, and various practice tools. Its user-friendly interface is beneficial for beginners, it allows them to learn the game at their own pace. These is also the option of a premium membership that provides access to more advanced features, such as video tutorials and a mentorship program.
Chess Tempo: ideal for tactics training, offering a vast selection of chess puzzles for different skill levels. The puzzles are based on real games, helping with applying tactical knowledge in a later practical game situation. The site also has digital books, a chess database, a playing area and endgame training.
Chessable: this site offers a wide range of online courses on various topics, from openings to specific strategies. The courses are provided by a range of creators, including top-ranking Grandmasters. The site is also known for its 'MoveTrainer' technology. A tool that helps the user to memorize chess-patterns, openings and endgames more efficiently.
ChessBase: the site offers up-to-date news coverage, feature articles, and instructional videos. You can also find a training area, which is a web version of ChessBase's popular database software, offering historical and annotated games for analysis and study.
Chess24: particularly notable for its live tournament broadcasting. It allows the user to follow major chess events in real-time, with commentary from experts. The site also provides interactive lessons and video series helmed by chess professionals. There is also a "playzone" where users can play against AI or other users.
These sites all provide something different and it is up to you to find the one that suits you best. They all cater to beginners as well as more experienced players, so you can find something that suits your skill level, way of learning and general preferences. Go explore!
