Chessboxing history

The sport came to life in 2003 through the work of the Dutch artist Iepe Rubingh, but how did he come up with the idea? Rubingh was inspired by the 1992 graphic novel "Froid Équateur" by Enki Bilal. In the graphic novel, set in a dystopian future, a version of chessboxing is the most popular sport in the world. After reading this, Rubingh, being an artist decided to create a live version of the sport as a piece of performance art. Luckily, he saw the potential of the sport and decided to create a real life version but without the dystopia. Had it not been for this stroke of brilliance, the history of chessboxing would have ended as a little known piece of performance art.

The rules of chessboxing

The match consists of 12 rounds, 6 rounds of chess and 5 rounds of boxing, starting with a chessround. There is a break of 1 minute between the rounds to allow the players to change their gear. A winner can be declared and the match thus ending in 4 different ways:
Checkmate - according to the rules of chess
Knockout - according to the rules of boxing
Technical knockout
Exceeding the time limit in chess
If all rounds are completed and none of the players have won through any of these ways, a sixth and last round of boxing takes place and the player with the most boxing points from the whole game wins. If after this last round it's still a tie, the player with the black pieces win.

There are separate referees for the rounds of chess and boxing, and this is essential. Judging any of the two events of the match require a specific set of expertise and focus and it not likely that a single induvidual would be able to not just have the required knowledge to judge the chess part and the boxing part, but also be able to keep focus and switch between these two seemlessly. By dividing the responsibilities it helps to ensure the integrity and fairness of the separate events and the whole match.

Chess and boxing rules

The rules of chess are the in general the same as in chess, the difference is that the players have a total of 12 minutes each to make all their moves. This done with the short rounds and by implementing a playing a variant of chess known as fast chess or as it also known, speed chess.

The rules for the rounds of boxing are the same as in regular boxing, with some exeptions. The match consists of five rounds, with sixth round added should there be a draw after the last round of chess. Additionally if the tournament requires is the competitors wear protective gear, such as gear to protect their heads. Usually this is only required in amateur events, but it is becoming more common in professional events. This is due to the fact that risk of injuries, especially head-injuries, are high and the WCBO takes the safety of their competitors very seriously. The weight-classes are also slightly different, but mostly based on the ones used in boxing. They may differ depending on a descision made by the organizers of a match or tournaments as well as if it is a professional or amateur event. Even if it would be great if chessboxing events attracted as many competitors as boxing, but it is still a relatively small and therefore doesn't have the same need for as many weight-classes. The weight-classes are not as strict as in boxing, since the competitors are not only judged on their weight but also their skill in chess. Still, the weight-classes are important since the competitors need to be evenly matched for the match to be fair.

Trivia and fun facts

  • The popular Netflix miniseries “The Queens Gambit” is dedicated to the founder Rubingh. He is credited as an “On-Set Chess Consultant” for the series.
  • In the 1992 graphic novel "Froid Équateur" by Enki Bilal the rules were slightly different. A whole boxing match was played before the chess game. Rubingh thought that this was impractical and adjusted the rules according to his vision.
  • The first chessboxing win was due to one of the players exceeding the time limit in chess.
  • The chess is played on board specially designed for chessboxing, exactly how it differs from a regular board is a little unclear. It has the same dimensions as a regular board, eight by eight squares, and the players have the same set up of pieces as in regular chess. The chess clock however is not the same as a regular chess clock, it is adapted for speed chess.
  • The founder Iepe Rubingh, was born on the 17th of August (1974) which by pure coincidence is the same day of birth as the legendary chess player Robert James Fischer... but perhaps more importantly it is also the birthday of the maker of this website.

The 10 golden rules of Chessboxing

What do you need to think of to become a successful chessboxer? These are the 10 golden rules every player should follow to make their chessboxing life easier, stated in the rule book of the World Chessboxing Organisation.

  1. Always remember you have decided for a combined sport, that means you've got to work twice as much and twice as hard as the others.
  2. Control yourself before your opponent does.
  3. If your position on the board is bad, make it a long game, when you're on top make it short.
  4. Never give up on the board, there will be a next round of boxing.
  5. Fight fair, points deduction hurts twice as much in chessboxing.
  6. Give it all in every boxing round, you'll have five minutes to regenerate afterwards.
  7. Never follow the chess speed of your opponent, control yourself and draw at your own speed.
  8. Know your opening repertoire with black and white, the quicker you are in the opening game the more time you'll have in the middle and end game.
  9. If you are superior in boxing, go for the KO, never count on a points win in chessboxing.
  10. Cool down after the boxing, never make your move too soon, you'll lose a few seconds on the clock, but it will pay off in the end.

Reflections and a conclusion

Apart from the reflections and thought about the sport, I would like to leave you with a personal note. You probably wonder why I have chosen to make a website about chessboxing. What is it about this sport that makes me want to share it? Do I practice it or is it something I admire from a distance? For starters, the story of how I found out about chessboxing is not very interesting. As a lover of knowledge, trivia and things that are curious, Ihave an app that gives me a piece of trivia/random fact every time I unlock my phone. One day, it was there, the concept of chessboxing was introduced to me. I was intrigued. I had to know more, so naturally I googled it and I was hooked. Why make an entire website about it ? For the full-stack developer program I am attending, I needed to create a website and combining my love of the sport and my love of coding seemed like brilliant idea (and in hindsight, it truly was). I also knew that it was a subject that would hopefully catch people's attention, judging by the reactions I have gotten when mentioning the sport to other people.

So many things about this sport interests and fascinates me. It makes me question why we admire greath strength or a superior intellect but why are these seldom shown as compatible? My interest is rooted deeper than this though. My connection with chess is quite personal since it makes me feel connected to my late grandfather. Sadly, I never got to know him since he passed away when I was 1.5 years old. Many times I have imagined how it would be to sit down with him, just talking and maybe, playing a game of chess or two. The accounts of him that I have been told always speak of his thirst for knowledge, his love of teaching and his kindness. Growing up, there was never a question me or my brother had that my father didn't answer. If he didn't know the answer straight away he'd bring out the encyclopedias (yes, before smartphones and wikipedia). My father's way of thinking, always looking for the best solution for a problem and seeing things much like puzzles or riddles waiting to be solved. I am sure he got from his father and he passed down to me and my brother. So, when it comes to chess my grandfather was a very passionate player. He would play chess through the mail, just sending moves back and forth in the post and moving pieces on separate boards he set up. Every room in my grandparents house had at least one chessboard, otherwise it would be incomplete. Even in his office he had two or three sets, just in case, he was a Professor of geology at Lund University* for many years and the door to his office was always open for collegues and students alike. A trait that both me and my father have is writing everything down, making lists about what lists I need to write. My grandfather meticulously recording each game and later turning these records into bound books. I have one of these, not because I study the moves but because it is a piece of my family legacy that I treasure.

* I find if extremely funny that our family name "Bergsten" in Swedish roughly translates to "Mountainstone", so he was a Professor of geology, named Professor Mountainstone...

The connection I have to boxing isn't personal in the same way but it means a lot to me all the same. My journey into boxing began around 17-19 years old. I took up the sport driven by a need to feel empowered after being bullied. Learning to defend myself, particularly as a female who might not appear to be much of a fighter, made me feel strong. Feeling strong and capable was something I desperately needed and I loved the sport. I had been partaking in many other sports growing up, but none of them gave me this feeling of empowerment. However, wrist injuries from there sports mainly tennis and handball, which lead to surgeries, made boxing a challenge and I couldn't continue, it was devastating. I am hoping that I may with some physcial therapy be able to return to the sport sometime, I don't think I will get too old, and if others think so well, I am really not bothered.

Yes, I promise I am wrapping this up now. With these two sports being important to me, each in their own way, it is not surprising that I would be drawn to chessboxing. Discovering chessboxing was a revelation. It beautifully melded two significant yet contrasting aspects of my life. Beyond the personal connection, I find the sport itself intriguing and entertaining. The blend of mental and physical challenges it presents is unique, and it's a sport I would have loved regardless. But the fact that it combines chess and boxing, both close to my heart for different reasons, makes it all the more special.
